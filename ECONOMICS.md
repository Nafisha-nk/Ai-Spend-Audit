# Economics

This tool only works for Credex if completed audits turn into qualified conversations about discounted AI credits. I would model a converted credit buyer as worth roughly $3,000 to $12,000 in first-year gross margin, depending on deal size. For example, assume a startup spends $4,000/month on eligible AI infrastructure, Credex helps them buy equivalent credits at a discount, and Credex earns a 10% margin on $48,000 annualized spend. That is $4,800 gross margin in year one. Bigger AI-native startups could be much higher; small teams under $500/month are mostly nurture leads.

The channel CAC can stay low because distribution is content/community-driven. Founder DMs cost time rather than cash: if 50 targeted DMs take 4 hours and produce 10 audits, CAC is the operator’s time divided by 10. A strong Hacker News or X teardown could produce 50-300 audits at near-zero cash cost. Community posts are similar, but they need specificity to avoid sounding like promotion. The highest-quality CAC is user interviews: each conversation can both improve the product and create a warm first audit.

A realistic funnel: 100 audit completions -> 25 email captures -> 8 high-savings leads -> 3 consultations booked -> 1 credit purchase. If the average purchase produces $4,800 first-year gross margin, then even a high manual follow-up cost is profitable. If the consultation-to-purchase rate drops below 10%, the tool needs better qualification or a clearer Credex callout. If email capture falls below 15%, the report is probably not valuable enough before the gate.

To drive $1M ARR in 18 months, the tool would need about $83k/month in recurring gross margin. At $4,800 first-year margin per customer, that is roughly 208 active equivalent customers per year, or 17-18 new customers per month. If 1% of completed audits become buyers, Credex needs around 1,800 completed audits/month. That is plausible only if public report sharing and teardown content create repeatable inbound distribution, not just one launch spike.
